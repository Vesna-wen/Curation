This exhibition charts a century of medical reckoning—where war’s devastation collided with human resolve to forge survival. From Crimea’s cholera-stricken barracks to World War I’s shattered psyches and World War II’s famine-scorched fields, King’s College London (KCL) and its institutions emerge as alchemists of crisis, transmuting trauma into disciplines now foundational to modern medicine: epidemiology, psychiatry, and nutrition.

The journey begins with Florence Nightingale’s 1855 “Nightingale Rose Diagram”, a spiraling indictment of preventable Crimean War deaths. Her revelation—that filth, not bullets, killed soldiers—ignited sanitation reforms at St Thomas’ Hospital, framing hospitals as spaces of science. This ethos bled into World War I, where Joseph Lister’s antiseptic surgery met the trenches’ carnage, while “shell shock” (a precursor to PTSD) emerged in soldiers’ fractured letters. Bethlem Hospital’s experimental therapies—a 1917 electrotherapy machine humming beside a nurse’s diary—ask: Can minds be mended like bones?

The final act turns to WWII, where KCL’s nutrition program countered famine’s silent siege. A 1943 ration book, scrawled with vitamin breakthroughs, mirrors Nightingale’s data-driven pragmatism, stitching eras together.

Like Werner Herzog’s “ecstatic truth”, this exhibition weaves fact and humanity into relics: a rusted Crimean scalpel rests near WWII dietary charts, their proximity a whisper—war’s wounds are universal, but so is healing’s stubborn light. By framing medicine as both artifact and allegory, the show interrogates: Can destruction’s lessons illuminate our present? KCL’s legacy answers not with certainty, but with tools forged in fire—a testament to hope’s fragile, relentless pulse.

Crimean War

W elcome to our online exhibition, exploring the profound contributions of King's College London to the history of war medicine. Florence Nightingale, known as the "Lady with the Lamp," made outstanding contributions during the Crimean War (1853–1856), where her pioneering efforts greatly improved hospital conditions and reduced mortality rates. She innovatively used data visualization to analyze soldier casualties, particularly through the creation of the Nightingale Rose Diagram, which vividly illustrated how the death rates from preventable diseases like typhus, cholera, and dysentery surpassed those from battle wounds. This visual representation played a crucial role in advocating for better sanitary practices and hospital management, emphasizing the critical impact of hygiene on soldier survival. Her groundbreaking work not only brought immediate improvements to medical care but also laid a vital foundation for the nursing profession, highlighting the importance of hygiene and systematic care.

A fter the war, Nightingale founded the Nightingale Training School for Nurses at St. Thomas' Hospital in 1860 to pass on her knowledge and establish nursing as a respected profession. This school was the first of its kind and became a model for nursing education worldwide. Over time, the Nightingale Training School evolved and became the predecessor of KCL's Florence Nightingale Faculty of Nursing, Midwifery & Palliative Care. KCL's Nightingale Faculty continues to uphold her legacy by advancing nursing education, research, and practice, ensuring that her pioneering spirit lives on. Through her work at St. Thomas' Hospital and her enduring legacy at KCL, Nightingale's contributions to war medicine and nursing continue to inspire and inform contemporary medical practices.

World War One

Shell Shock and KCL’s Contribution to PTSD Treatment

During World War I, "shell shock" emerged as a major psychological condition among soldiers, causing paralysis, tremors, and mental distress. Initially misunderstood as weakness, it is now recognized as an early form of PTSD. King’s College London played a crucial role in developing treatments, combining physiotherapy, psychotherapy, and rehabilitation, which laid the groundwork for modern PTSD care.

Bethlem Royal Hospital, known as "Bedlam," one of the world's oldest mental health institutions, became a center for treating war-related trauma during this time. KCL's collaboration with Bethlem advanced psychiatric research and treatment, laying the foundation for modern approaches to war-induced psychological conditions.

J oseph Lister's theory of aseptic surgery had a profound influence on wartime medicine during World War I. Lister’s 19th-century approach to antimicrobial surgery and aseptic practices provided surgeons with effective tools to treat battlefield injuries, especially open wounds from explosions and gunshots, which were highly prone to fatal infections.

Although antibiotics were not yet available during WWI, field surgeons adopted Lister's principles, using antiseptics to clean wounds and minimizing exposure to air, which significantly reduced infection rates and improved survival outcomes. According to KCL and the Royal Institution of Great Britain, KCL-trained medical professionals played a key role in implementing these techniques in war hospitals and on the front line. This not only increased the success rate of surgical procedures but also laid a foundation for modern trauma care and surgical techniques. Lister’s influence, reinforced by KCL’s research and training, shaped the development of wartime medicine and contributed to long-term advancements in surgical practices.

World War Two

D uring World War II, nutritionists identified key dietary challenges faced by soldiers and civilians under rationing. Researchers like John Yudkin and Elsie Widdowson investigated issues such as vitamin deficiencies, calorie requirements, and the effects of malnutrition. Their findings helped shape military rations and public food policies to maintain health despite food shortages.

After the war, Queen Elizabeth College, originally founded as King’s College for Women in 1908, became a leading institution in nutrition research. It was formally granted a Royal Charter in 1953 and later merged into King’s College London. Under Yudkin’s leadership, it established one of Europe’s first academic nutrition departments, expanding on wartime discoveries to explore obesity, heart disease, and metabolic health. The college’s work laid the foundation for modern nutritional science, influencing both military and civilian health policies globally.

Portrait of John Yudkin

John Yudkin was a pioneering British physiologist and nutritionist who discovered that riboflavin deficiency, rather than infection, caused a skin disease among African soldiers during WWII. He later established the first BSc degree in nutrition in Europe at Queen Elizabeth College, shaping modern nutritional science.
